User-centred Portfolio:
Enhancing leisure-place search in Hong Kong

From user research to final prototype, this portfolio documents how our team designed a calmer, more reliable place-discovery experience for users under stress.

Explore the project

Problem Statement

Why this project exists

Hong Kong users with heavy work or study pressure often want restorative breaks, but current search channels are noisy, generic, and inconsistent. This creates planning stress instead of relief.

Our design goal was to create an app that helps users discover genuinely suitable leisure places through trustworthy data, route certainty, and low-effort planning support.

Target users

  • People seeking quiet escapes during limited free time.
  • Users requiring accurate and up-to-date place information.
  • Users who value low-crowd, nature-oriented environments.

G1 · User Research & Analysis

User research process

We conducted contextual inquiries with 3 target users (30-45 minutes each), observing real mobile search behaviour, decision criteria, and pain points in realistic contexts.

01

P1 · Freelancer

Needed quiet environments and accurate opening/crowd information. Highlighted trust breakdown from outdated data.

Justification 1

Reliable, rich place details were prioritized because trust was repeatedly identified as a key adoption factor.

02

P2 · Student + part-time worker

Relied on social content but often found major mismatch between online impressions and real atmosphere.

Justification 2

Map and route assistance were treated as core features to reduce uncertainty and emotional friction.

03

P3 · Operations assistant

Required route precision and companionship support; surfaced safety and planning-anxiety concerns.

Justification 3

Planner and personalization functions were selected to reduce cognitive effort for users with low free time.

G2 · Design & Prototyping

Design and prototyping process

Scenario-driven design

  • Fast discovery of calm cafes during short breaks.
  • Shared planning for friend day trips via calendar flow.
  • Recommendation support with nearby facilities and crowd awareness.

Core tasks

  • Curated place search with maps, reviews, and contextual details.
  • Community hub for practical peer feedback.
  • Beforehand planning to cut decision time.

Layout clarity

We reduced crowding and improved hierarchy after internal wireframe critique.

Findability

Important actions and filters were made more visible to support task completion speed.

Planning flow

Route timeline and friend invitation mechanics were refined to lower coordination friction.

G3 · Evaluation & Final Prototype

Evaluation findings and impact

High severity

Critical actions like navigation were sometimes missed in long-scroll pages.

Safety gap: no clear off-course emergency support for remote exploration.

Medium severity

Comment content and map scale affected quick judgment and orientation confidence.

Implemented changes

Persistent navigation + pinned CTAs, assistance interface, and unified visual design system.

Reflection

What we learned and next steps

The strongest outcome was alignment between real user pain points and iteration priorities. Research led us to emphasize trust, safety, and discoverability rather than only adding features.

Next improvements

  • Improve live trust signals for opening status and crowd confidence.
  • Deepen emergency-flow testing with route deviation simulations.
  • Expand accessibility support for varied usage contexts.
